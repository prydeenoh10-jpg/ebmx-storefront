## What Actually Determines Top Speed

The number on a spec sheet is produced under controlled conditions. Understanding what drives it helps you predict real-world performance.

### Motor Power

More peak motor power means more force applied to the rear wheel. At top speed, all of that power is being used to overcome wind resistance and rolling friction. A more powerful motor can push through those resistances at a higher speed.

### Battery Voltage

A higher voltage battery delivers more energy per unit time to the motor. This is why the Ultra Bee's higher voltage system contributes directly to its higher top speed — the motor has more electrical energy to work with.

### Controller Settings

The controller is the brain of the drivetrain. It determines how much of the motor's theoretical capacity is actually delivered to the wheel. In Eco mode, the controller deliberately restricts this. In Sport mode, it opens up. An aftermarket or upgraded controller can allow greater current flow, effectively increasing the ceiling on what the motor can deliver.

### Rider Weight

Every extra kilogram on the bike has to be accelerated and maintained at speed against wind resistance. A lighter rider reaches top speed more easily and maintains it more comfortably, especially uphill. A heavier rider will see a reduction in practical top speed, particularly on any grade.

### Terrain and Conditions

Wind, surface, and gradient all play a role. Headwind, soft ground, and inclines all reduce the speed you'll see. For a realistic comparison across models, assume a flat sealed or compacted surface, neutral wind conditions, and a rider around 70–75kg.

## Putting the Speed Numbers in Context

Here's something worth sitting with before you fixate on top speed figures: 75 km/h on an electric dirt bike feels very different to 75 km/h on a road motorcycle.

On a road bike, you're seated in a more relaxed position, surrounded by a larger, heavier machine, and the tyres are designed for bitumen. On a Light Bee X or Ultra Bee, you're on knobby tyres, in an active trail riding posture, and the bike weighs a fraction of a road bike. At 75 km/h, a SurRon feels genuinely quick. It's not slow-bike fast — it's properly engaging.

The practical upshot is that for the vast majority of trail riding situations, 75 km/h from the Light Bee X is more than enough. You'll rarely approach that number on actual trail — the average speed through most single track is far lower. What you'll notice and appreciate more is how instantly the bike responds off the throttle, how well it climbs, and how easily it handles repeated acceleration-braking sequences.

Top speed is the headline number. Torque delivery and throttle response are what you actually ride.
