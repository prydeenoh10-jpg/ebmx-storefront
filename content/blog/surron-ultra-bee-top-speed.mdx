# SurRon Ultra Bee Top Speed

The SurRon Ultra Bee top speed in stock configuration is approximately 100 km/h on flat ground with a moderate-weight rider. That's a meaningful step up from the Light Bee X — and it comes from a genuinely bigger, more capable drivetrain rather than just a software adjustment. If you've been wondering whether the Ultra Bee is worth the step up in price and weight, the speed difference is real, but it's not the whole story.

## Why the Ultra Bee Is Faster Than the Light Bee X

The performance gap between these two bikes comes down to hardware, not just settings.

### Motor Output

The Ultra Bee runs a significantly more powerful motor than the Light Bee X.

> **⚠ VERIFY BEFORE PUBLISHING:** The peak motor output figures for both models — commonly cited as approximately 12.5 kW peak for the Ultra Bee and approximately 6 kW peak for the Light Bee X. These figures should be confirmed against current SurRon published specifications or EBMX technical documentation.

That's roughly double the peak power, which explains both the higher top speed and the considerably stronger acceleration the Ultra Bee delivers. The difference is felt most sharply in the mid-range: where a Light Bee X starts to flatten out, the Ultra Bee keeps pulling.

### Battery Voltage and Capacity

The Ultra Bee also runs a higher voltage battery system than the Light Bee X.

> **⚠ VERIFY BEFORE PUBLISHING:** The battery voltage of the Ultra Bee vs the Light Bee X — commonly described as 60V (LBX) vs 72V+ (Ultra Bee). Confirm the exact figures against current specifications for 2026 model year bikes.

Higher voltage means more energy delivered to the motor per second, which translates directly to more power. The Ultra Bee's battery is also larger in capacity, which supports sustained high-power output without the same degree of voltage sag as a smaller pack.

### Controller

The Ultra Bee's controller is matched to the larger motor and battery system. It's capable of managing the higher current required for peak power delivery, and its tuning is optimised for the more aggressive performance profile of the bike.

## Real-World Riding at Ultra Bee Speeds

A 100 km/h electric dirt bike changes the experience on open fire trails in a way that's hard to appreciate until you've ridden one.

At those speeds, the bike's geometry, tyre grip, and suspension start to matter in ways that feel more like a proper trail enduro bike than a fun play bike. The Ultra Bee's suspension is accordingly specced for higher-speed riding — it's noticeably more capable than the Light Bee X at the top end of the speed range.

### Where This Speed Is Usable

Open fire trails and forestry roads are where the Ultra Bee's top speed becomes practically relevant. On a clear, straight fire trail, being able to push beyond 80 km/h and still feel composed is a genuine advantage. The extra power also means that maintaining speed through moderate undulations — the kind that would slow a Light Bee X noticeably — happens almost invisibly on the Ultra Bee.

### Tight Technical Terrain

Here's the honest counterpoint: on tight, technical single track with lots of corners, roots, and climbs, you're almost never riding at more than 30–40 km/h regardless of what bike you're on. At those speeds, the Ultra Bee and the Light Bee X are much closer to each other. The Ultra Bee's extra power is still useful for technical climbing, but it's not the defining advantage it is on open runs.

## Weight Consideration: The Trade-Off

The Ultra Bee is heavier than the Light Bee X.

> **⚠ VERIFY BEFORE PUBLISHING:** The kerb weight of both models — commonly cited as approximately 50kg for the Light Bee X and approximately 65–70kg for the Ultra Bee. Confirm against current EBMX/SurRon specification sheets for 2026 models.

That weight difference is noticeable in a few specific situations:

**Acceleration from a standstill:** Despite having more peak power, the Ultra Bee's heavier mass means it doesn't necessarily feel dramatically faster off the mark than a Stage-upgraded Light Bee X. The power-to-weight ratio gap is smaller than the raw power figures suggest.

**Technical climbing:** More weight means more effort to get moving on steep, rocky climbs where you're barely rolling. The Ultra Bee compensates with torque, but it's a less flickable bike in genuine enduro terrain.

**Lifting and manhandling:** If you regularly have to move your bike around a ute tray, in and out of a shed, or over obstacles on foot, the extra 15–20kg is felt.

For open terrain, gravel tracks, and fast trail riding, the weight penalty is largely invisible — the extra power more than compensates. For tight single track and technical enduro, some riders genuinely prefer the lighter feel of the Light Bee X.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### Is the Ultra Bee legal on roads in Australia?

In stock configuration imported as an off-road bike, no. Road registration requires ADR compliance and a motorcycle licence. The Ultra Bee HP variant is designed with road use in mind in some markets, but Australian compliance is a separate process. See our full guide on [SurRon legality in Australia](/blog/are-surrons-legal-in-australia) for detail.

### How does the Ultra Bee compare to the Light Bee X?

The Ultra Bee is faster (approximately 100 km/h vs 75 km/h), more powerful, and heavier. It's the better bike for open trails and riders who want maximum performance. The Light Bee X is lighter, more flickable on tight terrain, and easier to manage for smaller or less experienced riders. The right choice depends on your riding style and terrain.
