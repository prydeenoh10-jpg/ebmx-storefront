## Understanding the Table: What "Top Speed" Actually Means

The figures above are measured under specific conditions: flat, firm surface, no wind, rider weight approximately 75kg, battery at full charge. In the real world, your numbers will differ. Here's why each condition matters.

### Flat Road, Ideal Conditions

A flat sealed or compacted surface eliminates gradient resistance. Any uphill grade — even a gentle 3–5% incline — will reduce top speed noticeably because the motor is using a portion of its output to climb rather than accelerate. Speed figures quoted for electric bikes and motorcycles are almost always measured on flat ground because it produces a consistent reference point, not because that's how you'll ride.

### A 75kg Rider

Rider weight has a proportionally larger effect on a light electric dirt bike than it does on a heavy road motorcycle. A 60kg rider may see top speeds at or slightly above the quoted figure. A 95kg rider may see figures 10–15% lower, particularly on any grade.

### Battery at Full Charge

Battery voltage drops as the pack discharges. A fully charged battery provides maximum voltage to the motor; a battery at 20% charge is providing noticeably less. In practical terms, you'll see full top speed for most of the ride, with a slight reduction in the last portion of the battery's range.

## Eco Mode vs Sport Mode: What's Actually Different

The two riding modes on SurRon bikes are not about two different physical states of the motor — the motor is the same either way. The difference is in what the controller allows the motor to receive.

**In Eco mode:** The controller limits the peak current delivered to the motor. This caps power output and therefore top speed, but it also produces a smoother, more linear throttle response. Battery consumption is lower, and range is extended. For technical single track, tight corners, and rider training, Eco mode is often the more useful setting — not because it's slow, but because the power delivery is more predictable.

**In Sport mode:** The controller allows full current delivery. Peak power is available, top speed is higher, and throttle response is sharper. The bike feels more aggressive. For open fire trails, fast terrain, and experienced riders who know the bike, Sport mode is where performance lives.

When you're a heavier rider, the motor has to do more work to accelerate that mass and maintain speed against wind resistance. The effect is nonlinear — going from 70kg to 80kg has less impact than going from 80kg to 100kg. Heavier riders will typically see a more pronounced speed reduction on any uphill grade, but the flat-road figures are more forgiving.

## How to Read These Numbers for Trail Riding

Here's the honest truth about top speed and trail riding: for most of the riding you'll actually do, the top speed figure is the least important number on the spec sheet.

Trail riding is a constant series of accelerations, decelerations, corners, and obstacles. Your average speed through genuine single track will rarely exceed 30–40 km/h, regardless of what the bike is theoretically capable of. The qualities that make a great trail bike are:

- **Torque at low speeds** — how confidently the bike climbs and hauls out of slow corners
- **Throttle response** — how immediately the power arrives when you open it up
- **Weight distribution and handling** — how the bike moves under you in technical terrain
- **Suspension** — how it handles the terrain impacts that occur at your actual riding speed

Top speed matters most on fire roads and open runs. If your riding is primarily tight single track, the difference between 75 km/h and 100 km/h bikes may be largely academic.

That said — having headroom matters. A bike that's comfortably below its top speed ceiling at your normal riding pace feels relaxed and controllable. A bike that's always at its limit feels strained. This is one reason why riders on mixed terrain often prefer the Ultra Bee's extra performance margin, even if they rarely use all of it.
